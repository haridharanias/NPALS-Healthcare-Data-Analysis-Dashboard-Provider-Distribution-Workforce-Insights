1. Problem Objective: 
This project delivers an end-to-end healthcare analytics solution using the National Post-Acute and Long-Term Care Study (NPALS) dataset, sourced from the U.S. Department of Health & Human Services. The analysis pipeline spans data cleaning and transformation in Microsoft Excel and Python through to interactive dashboard development in Power BI, producing a decision-ready intelligence layer over the U.S. post-acute and long-term care sector.
The project addresses the following analytical dimensions:
•	Provider Landscape Assessment: Mapping the distribution and density of care providers across seven post-acute and long-term care settings, including nursing homes, hospices, home health agencies, and residential care communities
•	Workforce Composition Analysis: Examining staffing levels, full-time equivalent employee counts, and nursing and social work workforce proportions across care settings to surface patterns in workforce intensity, role distribution, and sector-level staffing adequacy.
•	Service Utilization Profiling: Quantifying participant, resident, and patient volumes including daily averages and annual totals, to assess demand trends, facility-level throughput, and the divergence between provider supply and patient utilization following the COVID-19 period.
•	Operational Efficiency Benchmarking: Evaluating FTE hours per patient, resident, and participant as a proxy for care delivery efficiency, and benchmarking bed utilization and capacity stability across facility types to identify settings under strain or operating below optimal capacity.
The intended output is an interactive Power BI dashboard that enables healthcare administrators, policy analysts, and public health researchers to explore provider availability, workforce efficiency, and long-term care utilization trends supporting evidence-based planning and resource allocation decisions.

3. Problem Statement:
The United States post-acute and long-term care sector is navigating a period of significant structural pressure. Despite the availability of nationally representative survey data through the National Post-Acute and Long-Term Care Study (NPALS), there is a persistent gap between raw data availability and actionable insight. Provider counts, staffing levels, bed capacity, patient volumes, and efficiency ratios exist across seven distinct care settings -  nursing homes, hospices, home health agencies, residential care communities, adult day services centres, inpatient rehabilitation facilities, and long-term care hospitals, but without structured analysis, these figures cannot reveal the geographic inequities, workforce imbalances, or utilization trends that decision-makers require.
Specifically, this project addresses the below core analytical problems:
Workforce Capacity and Staffing Adequacy: Variation in full-time equivalent employee counts and nursing workforce proportions across care types suggests that staffing models are not consistently aligned with care intensity. Without a structured view of workforce distribution relative to patient and resident volumes, it is difficult to identify settings at risk of understaffing or those carrying inefficient staffing overhead.
Operational Efficiency and Utilization Misalignment: In several care settings, provider supply has grown while patient volumes per facility have declined, pointing to potential underutilization, fragmented referral networks, or unmet demand-side barriers. Simultaneously, bed utilization trends and FTE-to-patient ratios indicate that capacity planning decisions are being made without adequate performance benchmarking.
Addressing these problems requires a rigorous, data-driven analytical framework that transforms NPALS survey data into a coherent intelligence layer one capable of supporting evidence-based planning, equitable resource allocation, and informed policy decisions across the continuum of post-acute and long-term care.
